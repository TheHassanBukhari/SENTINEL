Part 4 of 5

SENTINEL: Every Structure in Context

Everything from Parts 1 to 3 now maps directly onto the epidemic system. This is where abstract structures become real decisions with real consequences.

SENTINEL stands for Spread and Exposure Network for Tracking, Isolation, and Neutralizing Epidemic Linked Nodes. The system models a live disease outbreak. Real people are being infected. Real contacts are being recorded. Every data structure has one specific job that no other structure in the system can do.

CSV data flows through DataLoader into all seven structures simultaneously. Dijkstra and Prim's run on top of the Graph whenever called.

S
Stack — The Case Investigation Log
One stack per patient, embedded inside the case object

In most systems a stack is a single global structure. In SENTINEL every patient carries their own private stack. This is one of the most important design decisions in the project.

Every time something happens to a patient, an event is pushed onto their personal stack. The stack becomes a self-contained forensic record of that case from day one to resolution.

What one stack node holds

  • Timestamp of the event
  • Day number of the outbreak when it occurred
  • Description: registered, tested, quarantined, status changed, condition worsened
  • Officer note recording the decision made at that moment

P003's personal stack. Pop from the top to see the most recent event. Traverse bottom to top for the full chronological story.

The stack serves two purposes simultaneously: it is a live log that grows as the case unfolds, and a forensic record that can be read backwards to reconstruct exactly what happened and when. Both uses emerge from the same LIFO structure.
Q
Queue — The Master Case Pipeline
FIFO — cases processed in the order they were recorded

Every patient registered in SENTINEL enters the master queue in arrival order. The queue is the spine of the entire system. Each node is not just a patient ID. It is a full case object containing everything about that patient, including their personal stack.

What each queue node carries

  • Patient ID, name, age, area code
  • Day of registration as an integer from 0 to current outbreak day
  • Status: positive, negative, quarantined, recovered
  • Risk score from the symptom questionnaire
  • Their personal Stack of investigation events

The day integer is particularly useful. The queue can be traversed and filtered by day to show exactly how many new cases appeared on any given day of the outbreak. This produces the epidemic growth curve directly from the data structure.

Double duty: The queue also powers the BFS wave simulation. Patient zero enters first. Their contacts are added to the back. Their contacts' contacts follow. Each processing round is one wave of spread. The FIFO order of the queue is what produces the wave-by-wave simulation naturally.
H
Heap — Risk Priority Engine
Highest risk patient always instantly accessible

At any moment during an outbreak, one question matters most: who should we act on right now? Not who arrived first. Not who has the highest ID number. Who is the most dangerous untested person in the system at this instant.

The heap answers this without any searching. The highest risk patient is always at the top.

Risk score breakdown

Every patient is scored on seven criteria at registration. Maximum total is 130 points.

When a patient's status changes, their score is recalculated and their position in the heap updates. The heap is never stale. It always reflects the current risk landscape.

AVL
AVL Tree — Date Range Search
Patients indexed by outbreak day, self-balancing

The AVL tree stores every patient indexed by their registration day. This enables something the queue and the hashmap fundamentally cannot do: range queries across a sorted timeline.

A health officer asks: show me every patient registered between day 5 and day 12. The AVL tree traverses only the relevant subtree and returns results without touching anything outside the range. The HashMap would require scanning every record. The queue would require walking the entire chain from front to back.

A range query for day 5 to 12 returns P009, P015, P021. Nodes outside the range are never touched. The tree also produces a sorted timeline via inorder traversal at no extra cost.

HM
HashMap — Dual Lookup System
Two hashmaps, two different problems solved

SENTINEL uses two separate hashmaps running in parallel. Each one solves a lookup problem that the other structures cannot handle efficiently.

Hashmap One — Person Profiles

Key: Patient ID like P003

Value: Pointer to full Case object

Direct access to any patient's complete profile, status, risk score, and investigation log in one step. Used by every menu operation that needs to find a specific person.

Hashmap Two — Location Index

Key: Area code like A1 or B2

Value: List of all patient IDs in that zone

Instantly reveals the case load per area. Detects hotspots. Compares zones. A single lookup shows which area needs testing teams deployed immediately.

When a new case is registered, both hashmaps update simultaneously. The person hashmap receives the new patient. The location hashmap adds their ID to their area's list.

Hotspot detection in one lookup: If area B2 returns 14 entries and area D4 returns 2, you know where the outbreak is concentrating without running a single query or traversal across any other structure.
G
Graph — The Contact Network
People as nodes, contacts as weighted edges

Every other structure stores data about individual patients. The graph stores relationships between them. Without the graph, SENTINEL is just a patient record system. With it, it becomes epidemic intelligence.

Every patient is a node. Every contact event between two patients is a weighted edge. Edge weight is calculated from duration of contact, location type (indoor carries higher risk than outdoor), and the day it occurred.

The contact graph. Edge thickness encodes risk weight. P003 (red) has multiple high-weight connections, identifying it as a super-spreader hub. Area codes on nodes enable zone-level analysis.

Because each node carries its area code, you can isolate the subgraph of any zone. All A1 nodes and their edges form zone A1's internal transmission network. An edge crossing from A1 to B2 signals inter-zone transmission beginning.

Dij
Dijkstra — Transmission Path Tracer
Most probable infection chain from patient zero to any individual

When a new person tests positive, the immediate question is not just who they are. It is how the virus reached them. Which specific chain of contacts is the most probable route from patient zero to this person?

Dijkstra runs on the contact graph with one deliberate inversion: higher edge weight means higher transmission risk, so we subtract the weight from a constant when calculating distance. This makes high-risk contacts appear as short distances to Dijkstra. The shortest path it finds is therefore the highest-probability infection chain.

The output is a precise sequence. P001 infected P002 at a market on day 1. P002 infected P011 at a clinic on day 3. P011 infected the new patient at a gathering on day 7. Every link in the chain is mathematically the strongest candidate for that transmission step.

Not a guess. Dijkstra provides a mathematical guarantee: given the edge weights, this is the optimal path. Investigators know exactly who to interview, who to test next, and where the critical link in the chain is.
MST
Prim's — Minimum Containment Network
Surgical quarantine targeting using graph mathematics

When a cluster forms, you cannot quarantine everyone. Resources are finite. Prim's finds the minimum set of contact links holding the entire cluster together.

These links are the bridges between sub-groups. The people who connect otherwise separate pockets of infection. Quarantine these specific bridge individuals and you do not just isolate them. You split the cluster into disconnected fragments that can no longer infect each other.

This is the difference between quarantining 40 people by intuition and quarantining 8 people by graph structure and achieving the same containment outcome.

Prim's output is an action list. Each item is two patient IDs and the risk weight of the contact between them. Break the highest-weight links first and the cluster collapses fastest. This is surgical containment, not blanket quarantine.

A Full Scenario: Day 9, New Patient Arrives
All seven structures in motion simultaneously

A new patient walks into the testing center on day 9 of the outbreak. Here is the exact sequence of what SENTINEL does:

  1. A new Case object is created with their profile, symptom answers, area code, and day 9.
  2. Risk score is calculated from the seven criteria. Result: 90 out of 130.
  3. Case is enqueued into the master Queue. They join the pipeline in arrival order.
  4. Patient ID and score 90 are inserted into the Heap. They land near the top.
  5. Patient ID and day 9 are inserted into the AVL Tree. Tree rebalances if needed.
  6. Patient ID maps to their Case object in the person HashMap.
  7. Patient ID is added to their area code's list in the location HashMap.
  8. A graph node is created for this patient. Edges are drawn to known contacts with risk weights.
  9. An initial event is pushed to their personal Stack: Day 9, registered, score 90, intake note.
  10. Dijkstra traces the transmission path from patient zero to this patient and prints the chain.
  11. Prim's recalculates the minimum containment network on the updated graph.

Eleven operations. All seven structures updated. The system now knows who this person is, where they rank by risk, when they arrived, which area they belong to, how the virus reached them, and what their presence changes about the containment strategy.
